The Fidelity–Safety Gap in Neural Wikidata Repair

A standalone research overview of executable constraint factors as a neuro-symbolic representation for Wikidata repair, with evidence that historical edit imitation, symbolic safety, and non-vacuous repair quality must be evaluated as separate objectives.

1. Central Thesis

Central thesis. In neural Wikidata repair, better imitation of historical curator edits is not equivalent to safer symbolic repair. Executable constraint factors make this gap visible: they substantially improve edit-slot prediction, but they do not by themselves reduce secondary symbolic regressions. Conversely, optimizing local post-edit constraint satisfaction can collapse into a trivial delete-focus policy. The central contribution is a fidelity-safety evaluation framework rather than a claim of solved safe-repair architecture.

The project is an empirical and methodological contribution to knowledge graph repair: it introduces a constraint-aware neural representation, evaluates it against symbolic post-edit criteria, and shows that the common shortcut from historical supervision to repair quality is unreliable. The results are valuable precisely because they reveal a failure mode that would be hidden by edit-slot F1 alone.

Supported claim

Executable constraint factors improve historical edit imitation, learn meaningful constraint-satisfaction signals, and expose a measurable fidelity–safety gap in neural repair.

Scope limit

The evidence does not support the claim that executable factors, by themselves, solve safe Wikidata repair or broadly dominate simpler baselines on symbolic safety.

2. Abstract

Collaborative knowledge graphs such as Wikidata use property constraints to flag suspicious statements, yet repairing these violations is not merely a matter of maximizing local constraint satisfaction. Historical curator edits provide useful supervision, but curator imitation and symbolic safety are distinct objectives: a repair can resemble historical behavior while preserving or introducing other violations, and a globally satisfying edit can remove evidence rather than repair it. This report studies this fidelity–safety gap in neural Wikidata repair.

We introduce executable constraint factors, a neuro-symbolic graph representation in which locally applicable Wikidata constraints are encoded as typed factor nodes. These factors learn differentiable approximations of constraint satisfaction and send role-conditioned pressure messages to the subject, predicate, object, or value nodes in their scope. The model is trained on historical repair supervision and evaluated by applying predicted edits to the pre-repair graph, then rechecking local symbolic constraints.

Experiments on a stratified Wikidata repair benchmark show that executable factors substantially improve historical edit-slot prediction, raising Micro-F1 from 0.5434 to 0.6785 over a passive constraint-context baseline. However, this representation gain does not automatically translate into safer repairs: the factorized imitation model performs worse than the passive baseline on aggregate local satisfaction and secondary-regression metrics. A global-only reranking endpoint achieves the best local satisfaction and matches a delete-focus heuristic on aggregate evaluation metrics, revealing a satisfaction-by-deletion degeneracy. Diagnostic pressure-masking experiments show that factor pressure is causally important for prediction, but mostly supports historical imitation and primary repair behavior rather than secondary no-regression. These findings motivate evaluating KG repair along separate axes of historical fidelity, primary repair intent, secondary no-regression, and non-vacuous evidence preservation.

Positioning: executable constraint factors are the instrument that exposes the fidelity-safety gap. They are important as a representation and diagnostic mechanism, while safe repair remains a decision-level problem requiring explicit no-regression and non-vacuity controls.

3. Problem, Scope, and Non-Claims

Wikidata is a collaborative knowledge graph with soft, evolving property constraints. A constraint violation marks a statement as suspicious, not necessarily false. Human curators may fix the data, add missing context, remove an erroneous statement, tolerate an exception, or implicitly follow community conventions that are not fully captured by a local symbolic validator.

This project studies a restricted but measurable repair setting. Given a violated statement, local graph context, locally applicable constraints, and a historical curator edit, the model predicts a local add/delete edit represented by six slots: subject_add, predicate_add, object_add, subject_delete, predicate_delete, and object_delete. The predicted edit is then applied to the local pre-repair graph and evaluated with symbolic constraint checks.

Historical fidelity

Does the model predict the same edit slots as the historical curator action? This is measured with edit-slot precision, recall, and Micro-F1.

Primary repair intent

Does the predicted edit make the originally violated constraint satisfied after the edit is applied?

Secondary no-regression

Does the edit avoid breaking other constraints that were satisfied before the repair?

Non-vacuous repair

Does the edit improve the graph rather than merely deleting the evidence that made the violation observable?

Non-claim. This project does not claim full Wikidata repair. It studies local statement-level repair under a six-slot add/delete abstraction. Qualifiers, references, ranks, exceptions, constraint evolution, and T-Box repair are outside this local repair task.

4. Scientific Contributions

The contribution is organized around four defensible claims that connect the method, the metrics, and the observed failure modes.

Constraint factors improve historical edit prediction. Representing locally applicable constraints as typed factor nodes improves Micro-F1 from 0.5434 to 0.6785 compared with the passive constraint-context baseline.
Better imitation does not imply symbolic safety. The factorized imitation model improves edit-slot fidelity but performs worse than the passive baseline on aggregate local satisfaction, secondary improvement, secondary regression, and disruption.
Global local satisfaction is an unsafe optimization target by itself. The global-only endpoint matches a delete-focus heuristic on aggregate evaluation metrics, showing that local symbolic satisfaction can be maximized by removing the violating statement rather than repairing it in a semantically useful way.
Factor diagnostics reveal where the safety mechanism fails. Learned factors predict constraint satisfaction well and affect model decisions, but secondary-only pressure barely changes predictions. The architecture uses constraint pressure mostly for imitation and primary repair behavior, not for secondary no-regression.

5. Method: Executable Constraint Factors

The method is central, and its role is specific. The contribution is not a generic factor-graph neural network. Existing neural factor-graph methods already use variable nodes, factor nodes, and learned message passing. The specific contribution here is to turn concrete Wikidata constraint instances into typed neural factors inside a local repair graph, while retaining symbolic validators for post-edit evaluation.

Repair instance

Local repair example \[ x = \left(G^{-}, \Delta^{\star}, C_{\mathrm{local}}, c^{\star}\right), \qquad C_{\mathrm{local}} = \{c^{\star}, c_1, \ldots, c_m\} \]

G- is the pre-repair local graph, Delta* is the historical curator edit, c* is the primary violated constraint, and C_local contains the primary and secondary constraints visible in the local context.

Factor execution

Typed neural constraint factor \[ \begin{aligned} z_c^{(k)} &= \operatorname{AGG}_{t(c)} \left(\{h_v^{(k)} : v \in \operatorname{Scope}(c)\}\right) \\ \hat{s}_c^{(k)} &= \sigma\!\left(f_{t(c)}\!\left(z_c^{(k)}\right)\right) \\ \operatorname{viol}_c^{(k)} &= 1 - \hat{s}_c^{(k)} \\ m_{c \to v}^{(k)} &= g_{t(c), r(v,c)} \!\left( z_c^{(k)}, h_v^{(k)}, \operatorname{viol}_c^{(k)} \right) \end{aligned} \]

Each local constraint c has a family t(c), a scope of variable nodes, and role-specific connections to subjects, predicates, objects, or values. The factor head estimates satisfaction, and the resulting pressure message is sent back to scoped variables. The term executable is used in this bounded sense: the neural factor is a learned approximation of constraint satisfaction, while the grounded post-edit evaluation is still performed by symbolic constraint checks.

Pressure update

Constraint-pressure message passing \[ h_v^{(k+1)} = \operatorname{GNN}\!\left(h_v^{(k)}, \mathcal{N}(v)\right) + \lambda \sum_{c:\, v \in \operatorname{Scope}(c)} m_{c \to v}^{(k)} \]

This residual pressure term is the architectural mechanism under test. If removing pressure at inference time sharply changes predictions, then the factor pathway is causally involved. If secondary-only pressure barely changes predictions, then the model is not effectively using secondary constraints for no-regression decisions.

6. Evaluation: Separate Fidelity from Safety

The evaluation is the main scientific lens of the report. A predicted edit is applied to the pre-repair graph, and symbolic constraints are rechecked after the edit. This separates what the model predicts from what the edit does.

Post-edit symbolic evaluation \[ G^{\Delta} = \operatorname{Apply}\!\left(G^{-}, \Delta\right), \qquad S_c(G) \in \{0,1\} \]
Local symbolic safety metrics \[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{PrimaryFix} &= S_{c^{\star}}\!\left(G^{\Delta}\right) \\ \operatorname{LocalSatisfaction} &= \frac{1}{|C_{\mathrm{local}}|} \sum_{c \in C_{\mathrm{local}}} S_c\!\left(G^{\Delta}\right) \\ \operatorname{SRR} &= \frac{ \left|\{c \neq c^{\star} : S_c(G^{-}) = 1 \land S_c(G^{\Delta}) = 0\}\right| }{ \left|\{c \neq c^{\star} : S_c(G^{-}) = 1\}\right| } \\ \operatorname{SIR} &= \frac{ \left|\{c \neq c^{\star} : S_c(G^{-}) = 0 \land S_c(G^{\Delta}) = 1\}\right| }{ \left|\{c \neq c^{\star} : S_c(G^{-}) = 0\}\right| } \end{aligned} \]

The label GFR denotes local post-edit constraint satisfaction over the extracted constraint set. Calling it simply “global” is risky because it is not global over Wikidata, and because high satisfaction can be produced by vacuous deletion.

Metric caveat. Local satisfaction is not repair quality. The G0/H1 result shows that this metric can reward deleting the violating statement. It is one diagnostic axis, not the primary optimization target.

Finding 1: factors help imitation

A1 improves Micro-F1 from 0.5434 to 0.6785. This is the cleanest positive result for executable factors.

Finding 2: imitation is not safety

A1 is worse than B0 on GFR, SIR, SRR, and disruption. The representation helps predict curator-like edits but does not automatically reduce collateral symbolic damage.

Finding 3: direct safety objectives are not enough yet

M1D is the best practical compromise among factorized variants, but the aggregate result is not strong enough to claim broad symbolic-safety dominance.

Finding 4: satisfaction can be degenerate

G0 matches H1 on aggregate evaluation metrics. This is one of the report's most important empirical observations.

8. Diagnostics: What the Factors Actually Do

The diagnostics explain the mechanism behind the headline results. They are not additional leaderboard metrics. They are tests of whether the factor pathway learns meaningful constraint information, whether that information changes repair predictions, and whether the change is driven by the primary violated constraint or by secondary constraints in the local context.

This matters because the main result has two parts that need to be reconciled: executable factors improve edit-slot imitation, but they do not automatically improve aggregate symbolic safety. The diagnostics show that the factors are not decorative: they learn satisfaction signals and affect predictions. However, the pressure is mostly used for historical imitation and primary repair behavior.

Factor semantics

Factor semantics measures whether the learned factor heads can predict symbolic constraint satisfaction. Each factor node outputs a satisfaction probability for its local constraint instance. The diagnostic compares those neural predictions against labels produced by the symbolic validators.

The State column identifies which graph state is being predicted. pre is the local graph before any repair is applied. post_gold is the local graph after applying the historical curator edit. Strong results on pre show that the model recognizes existing local constraint state; strong results on post_gold show that it also models the symbolic state associated with historical repairs.

Calibration caveat. Weighted F1 is high partly because many factor labels are likely imbalanced. AUROC, AUPRC, and calibration are therefore important companion statistics for sparse or highly skewed constraint families.

Pressure masking

Pressure masking is an inference-time intervention. The same trained model is evaluated again after removing selected factor-to-variable pressure messages. If performance changes substantially, the removed pressure pathway was causally important for the prediction. If performance barely changes, the model was not relying strongly on that pathway.

No factor pressure removes all executable-factor feedback, leaving the model without factor-to-variable pressure messages. Secondary-only pressure keeps pressure from secondary constraints while removing the primary/focal pressure pathway. This isolates whether non-primary constraints can influence the edit decision on their own. The deltas report metric change relative to the unmasked model; negative values mean the intervention hurt performance.

Diagnostic conclusion. The factorized architecture learns and uses constraint pressure, but the pressure is captured primarily by the imitation objective. The mechanism does not make secondary constraints strong decision-level controls. This explains why better Micro-F1 does not become better symbolic safety.

9. Scientific Value and Positioning

The scientific value is not that the proposed model wins every metric. The value is that the project reveals a structural evaluation problem in learned KG repair. Historical edit logs are attractive supervision, but they are incomplete proxies for repair quality. Symbolic satisfaction is attractive as an objective, but it can be satisfied vacuously. A credible repair evaluation must therefore measure both and explain their conflicts.

For knowledge graph repair

Repair systems need evaluation by collateral symbolic effects, not only by agreement with historical edits or by post-edit satisfaction of the primary violation.

For neuro-symbolic learning

The results show that symbolic structure can be learned and used by a neural model, while also showing that representation-level symbolic awareness is insufficient for safe decision making.

For benchmark design

The benchmark is a multi-axis evaluation surface that separates imitation, primary fix, secondary no-regression, local satisfaction, and edit disruption.

For future repair systems

The findings motivate constrained reranking, candidate validity checks, calibration, non-vacuity metrics, and stronger secondary-pressure routing.
